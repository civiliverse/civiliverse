**卷首引文** — 「鞍上须备两只铁镫。」——《战略》（托名莫里斯皇帝，约600年）
*"The saddle should have two iron stirrups." — Strategikon, attributed to the Emperor Maurice (c. 600)*

**定位句** — 一只铁环，曾被认为解释了整个中世纪。它没有；但为什么会有人这么想，比它本身更值得一说。
*An iron ring once thought to explain the whole Middle Ages. It does not—and why anyone thought it might is worth more than the ring itself.*

**历史叙述** — 马镫的功用是给骑者一个稳定的支点。无镫骑乘并非不能作战——斯基泰人、帕提亚人与匈奴均以骑射闻名——但骑者只能靠双腿夹持与鞍具承力，难以在高速冲击中承受长矛触敌那一瞬的反作用。单边上马用的绳环，在印度与中国的早期图像中均有讨论；成对的金属马镫，目前最早的确切实物出自中国东北的4世纪墓葬，包金铜的木芯镫见于三燕与高句丽遗存，其中辽宁北票冯素弗墓（约415年）一对年代尤为确切。此后马镫随草原骑马民族西行：阿瓦尔人于6世纪携入潘诺尼亚；约600年成书的拜占庭兵书《战略》已把「铁镫两只」列为骑兵制式装备。法兰克世界的马镫实物见于8世纪，与查理·马特的时代大致同期。中国方面，唐代马镫已属常制，形制自圆环演为踏板宽平的样式。
*The stirrup's function is to give the rider a fixed purchase. Riding without one is by no means unfighting—Scythians, Parthians, and Xiongnu were all famed horse-archers—but the rider must hold by his legs and by the saddle, and can hardly absorb the reaction at the instant a couched lance strikes at speed. A single mounting-loop is discussed in early Indian and Chinese images; for paired metal stirrups, the earliest certain finds are from fourth-century tombs in northeastern China, gilt bronze over a wooden core, among the remains of the Xianbei states and Koguryŏ—the pair from the tomb of Feng Sufu at Beipiao in Liaoning (c. 415) being especially securely dated. From there stirrups moved west with steppe horsemen: the Avars brought them into Pannonia in the sixth century, and the Byzantine Strategikon of about 600 already lists two iron stirrups as issue equipment for cavalry. Frankish finds date to the eighth century, roughly the age of Charles Martel. In China the stirrup was standard by the Tang, its form evolving from a plain ring to a broad flat tread.*

**史论** — 1962年，林恩·怀特在《中世纪技术与社会变迁》中提出了一条漂亮的因果链：马镫使重装冲击骑兵成为可能，重骑兵昂贵，供养重骑兵需要土地，分封土地便产生了封建制——一只铁环，解释了欧洲一千年的社会结构。这个论题的命运值得每个写技术史的人记住。巴克拉赫等人的反驳几乎逐环拆解了它：加洛林军队中的骑兵常常下马步战；冲击战术的成熟晚于马镫传入数百年；采邑与依附关系在马镫之前已有其法律与社会来源；而马镫所到之处并未普遍长出封建制——中国有马镫，没有采邑骑士。今天史学界对怀特论题的处置是明确的：**否弃其结论，保留其问题。** 那个问题是：一件器物究竟能改变多少？答案不是「零」——马镫确实改变了人在马背上能做什么；但从「能做什么」到「社会长成什么样」，中间隔着地权、法律、财政、战争形态与许多具体的人的选择，这些都不是一只铁环能替它们决定的。怀特留给这个学科最大的遗产，是他犯的这个错足够清楚——清楚到成了此后一切技术决定论的标尺。
*In 1962 Lynn White Jr. proposed, in Medieval Technology and Social Change, a handsome chain of causes: the stirrup made mounted shock combat possible; heavy cavalry was expensive; maintaining it required land; granting land produced feudalism—one iron ring accounting for a thousand years of European social structure. The fate of that thesis is worth remembering by anyone who writes about technology. Bernard Bachrach and others took it apart nearly link by link: Carolingian cavalry often dismounted to fight; mature shock tactics postdate the stirrup's arrival by centuries; benefice and dependence had legal and social sources of their own before it; and feudalism did not sprout wherever stirrups went—China had stirrups and no enfeoffed knights. The discipline's settlement is clear: reject the conclusion, keep the question. The question is how much a single object can change. The answer is not "nothing"—the stirrup did change what a person can do on horseback—but between what can be done and what a society becomes lie landholding, law, revenue, the form of war, and a great many particular human choices, none of which an iron ring decides for them. White's largest bequest to the field is that his error was clear enough to become the measure of every technological determinism after it.*
