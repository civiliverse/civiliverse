**卷首引文** — 「天花在我们这里如此致命而普遍，在这里却因发明了『接种』而毫无危害。」——蒙塔古夫人自阿德里安堡致友人书（1717）
*"The small-pox, so fatal and so general amongst us, is here entirely harmless by the invention of engrafting." — Lady Mary Wortley Montagu, from Adrianople (1717)*

**定位句** — 人类第一次成功地预防一种疾病，用的是一个当时谁也解释不了的办法。
*The first disease humanity successfully prevented was prevented by a method no one could then explain.*

**历史叙述** — 天花在有疫苗之前是一种寻常的死亡原因，18 世纪的欧洲每年约夺去四十万人性命，幸存者多留瘢痕，部分失明。人痘接种的原理是取轻症患者的痘痂或痘浆，经鼻或经皮划入健康人体，使其得一次可控的轻症从而终身免疫。中国至迟在 16 世纪已有明确记载，行「吹鼻」之法，并有选取「熟苗」（经数代传递而毒力减弱者）的经验；奥斯曼帝国与印度另有其法，多以臂上划痕行之。1717 年英国驻奥斯曼大使夫人蒙塔古在阿德里安堡目睹此法，为自己的儿子接种，返英后又为女儿接种并大力推行，1721 年伦敦以死囚与孤儿试行成功后，接种在上层社会流行开来。风险是明确的：接种者得的是真天花，约百分之一二死亡，且在发病期具传染性，可引发疫情。1796 年，格洛斯特郡乡村医生詹纳注意到当地长期流传的说法——挤奶女工手上生过牛痘的，不会得天花。他取一名挤奶女工手上的牛痘浆，接种八岁男孩菲普斯，六周后以天花痘浆攻毒，男孩未发病。1798 年他自费出版《牛痘的成因与效果之探究》，vaccination 一词自拉丁语 vacca（牛）而来。此法风险远低于人痘，数十年间传遍欧洲、美洲与亚洲。其机理则久悬未决——直到 1880 年代巴斯德以减毒法制备鸡霍乱与炭疽疫苗，并出于对詹纳的敬意把 vaccine 一词推广为一切此类制剂的通名，免疫的道理才随细菌学与免疫学的建立而逐步给出。1967 年世卫组织启动全球根除计划，以监测—围堵接种取代普种，1977 年索马里出现最后一例自然感染，1980 年宣布根除。
*Before there was a vaccine, smallpox was an ordinary cause of death, killing perhaps four hundred thousand a year in eighteenth-century Europe and leaving most survivors scarred and some blind. Variolation took scab or fluid from a mild case and introduced it through the nose or through the skin, to produce one controlled and usually mild illness and lifelong immunity thereafter. China has clear records by the sixteenth century of the insufflation method, together with the practice of selecting "matured" material passaged until its virulence had fallen; the Ottoman Empire and India had their own methods, generally by incision in the arm. In 1717 Lady Mary Wortley Montagu, wife of the British ambassador, saw the practice at Adrianople, had her son inoculated, and on returning home had her daughter done and pressed the method vigorously; after trials on condemned prisoners and orphans succeeded in London in 1721, inoculation spread through polite society. The risk was plain: the inoculated took true smallpox, one or two in a hundred died, and while ill they were infectious and could start an outbreak. In 1796 Edward Jenner, a country physician in Gloucestershire, took note of the local saying that milkmaids who had had cowpox on their hands did not take smallpox. He took matter from a milkmaid's hand, inoculated the eight-year-old James Phipps, and six weeks later challenged him with smallpox; the boy did not sicken. His Inquiry into the Causes and Effects of the Variolae Vaccinae, published at his own expense in 1798, gave the practice its name, from the Latin vacca, a cow. Far less dangerous than variolation, it spread within decades through Europe, the Americas, and Asia. The reason it worked remained open—until in the 1880s Pasteur prepared attenuated vaccines against fowl cholera and anthrax and, in deference to Jenner, extended the word vaccine to all such preparations, and the account of immunity came gradually with bacteriology and immunology. The WHO began its global eradication programme in 1967, replacing mass vaccination with surveillance and ring vaccination; the last natural case occurred in Somalia in 1977, and eradication was declared in 1980.*

**史论** — 疫苗接种在本图谱中的位置，主要不在它救了多少人（虽然那是个极大的数字），而在它把「技术与科学孰先孰后」这个问题摆得格外清楚。教科书式的次序是：科学发现原理，技术加以应用。疫苗的次序恰好相反——人痘在中国行之数百年，牛痘在英格兰行之百年，其间无人知道免疫为何物；理论不是它的前提，而是它提出的问题。这类例子在技术史上远比反例多：蒸汽机先于热力学，冶炼先于化学，育种先于遗传学。**说「技术是应用科学」，是把一段例外当成了通例。** 第二层关于知识的来路。詹纳的线索来自乡间挤奶女工的经验，蒙塔古的来自奥斯曼妇女的日常实践，中国的熟苗选择法来自世代接种者的观察——三处都是**未被记入学术体系的实作知识**，而它们各自都对了。这与 `maize-domestication` 条所记的碱法蒸煮是同一类事：正规知识体系之外的操作常识，其可靠性常被低估，而其失传的代价常被低估得更多。第三层须诚实：这段历史里也有不该被略过的部分——1721 年伦敦的人痘试验以死囚与孤儿为受试者，其「同意」在今天的标准下不成立（见 `research-ethics`）；牛痘推行期间的强制接种法在英国引发了持续数十年的抵制运动，其中既有对国家权力的合理疑虑，也有被后来的证据否弃的主张。**一项好技术的历史，不会因为它好而变得干净。**
*Vaccination's place in this graph lies less in the number of lives it saved—vast though that is—than in how plainly it sets out the question of whether technology or science comes first. The textbook order is that science finds the principle and technology applies it. Vaccination runs the other way: variolation was practised for centuries in China and cowpox for a century in England while no one knew what immunity was. Theory was not its precondition but the question it posed. Such cases are far commoner in the history of technology than the converse: the steam engine preceded thermodynamics, smelting preceded chemistry, breeding preceded genetics. To say that technology is applied science is to mistake an exception for the rule. A second layer concerns where knowledge comes from. Jenner's clue came from the experience of country milkmaids, Montagu's from the everyday practice of Ottoman women, and the Chinese selection of matured material from generations of inoculators' observation—three bodies of working knowledge unrecorded by any learned system, and each of them right. This is of a kind with nixtamalization in maize-domestication: the reliability of operational common sense outside formal knowledge is habitually underrated, and the cost of losing it is underrated further. A third layer requires honesty. There is a part of this history that should not be passed over: the London trials of 1721 used condemned prisoners and orphans as subjects, whose consent would not stand by any present standard (see research-ethics); and compulsory vaccination during the spread of the cowpox method provoked decades of resistance in Britain, comprising both reasonable misgivings about state power and claims that later evidence refuted. The history of a good technology does not become clean by virtue of its being good.*
