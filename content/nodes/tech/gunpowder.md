**卷首引文** — 「有以硫黄、雄黄合硝石并蜜烧之，焰起烧手面及尽屋舍者。」——《真元妙道要略》（约9世纪）
*"Some have heated together sulphur, realgar, saltpetre and honey; smoke and flames result, so that their hands and faces have been burnt, and even the whole house burned down." — Zhenyuan Miaodao Yaolüe, c. 9th c.*

**定位句** — 世界上第一条关于火药的记载，是一句劝人别这么干的安全提示。
*The world's first written notice of gunpowder is a warning not to make it.*

**历史叙述** — 火药出自炼丹炉。唐代方士为求长生而合炼硝石、硫黄与炭属之物，屡屡失火，遂有《真元妙道要略》一类丹书中的告诫。北宋庆历四年（1044）成书的《武经总要》录有「毒药烟球」「蒺藜火球」「火炮」三方，硝、硫、炭配比明确，是现存最早的成文火药配方——其硝含量尚低，燃烧猛烈而不足以爆裂，正合当时「纵火」而非「炸城」的用法。此后火器沿两条路走：一是燃烧与烟毒，二是管形射击。南宋出现以竹为筒、发射「子窠」的突火枪；元代改用铜铁铸铳，现存至正十一年（1351）等纪年铳可证。13世纪中叶前后，火药知识随蒙古西征与欧亚商路抵达伊斯兰世界与欧洲，14世纪欧洲开始铸炮，并在15世纪发展出颗粒化火药。
*Gunpowder came out of the alchemist's furnace. Tang adepts seeking immortality repeatedly set their workshops alight while heating saltpetre, sulphur, and carbonaceous matter—hence the cautions preserved in alchemical texts such as the Zhenyuan Miaodao Yaolüe. The Wujing Zongyao, completed in 1044, records three formulas—"poison-smoke ball," "caltrop fire-ball," and "fire cannon"—with explicit proportions of nitrate, sulphur, and charcoal: the oldest written recipes extant. Their nitrate content is low, giving fierce burning rather than detonation, which suits the contemporary purpose of setting fires rather than breaching walls. Firearms then developed along two lines: incendiary and toxic smoke on one hand, tube-launched projectiles on the other. The Southern Song produced the fire-lance, a bamboo tube shooting pellets; the Yuan cast barrels in bronze and iron, attested by dated surviving pieces such as that of 1351. Around the mid-thirteenth century the knowledge reached the Islamic world and Europe along the routes opened by Mongol campaigns and trade; Europe was casting cannon by the fourteenth century and had corned powder by the fifteenth.*

**史论** — 火药常被当作「中国发明、西方发扬」的样板案例，用以证明某种文明性格的高下。这个叙事经不起细看。宋元的火器谱系连续而繁密，从火箭到火铳并无停顿；欧洲的差别不在于「更会用」，而在于它处在一个城堡林立、政权破碎、彼此以攻城相持的环境里——攻城的需求把火炮的口径与铸造工艺一路推了上去。技术的走向由它落地的战争形态决定，而非由发明者的国族禀赋决定。真正值得记住的另一层是它的出身：追求长生的实验，最终产出了缩短寿命的手段。这不是反讽的修辞，而是技术史的常态——目的与后果之间从来没有直路。
*Gunpowder is often used as the showcase of "invented in China, perfected in the West," and thence as evidence about the character of civilizations. The story does not survive inspection. The Song–Yuan lineage of firearms is continuous and dense, running from fire-arrow to hand cannon without pause; Europe's difference lay not in greater ingenuity but in its setting—a landscape of castles, fragmented sovereignties, and sieges. Siege demand drove up bore and casting technique. Where a technology goes is decided by the form of war it lands in, not by the national endowments of its inventors. The other thing worth remembering is its parentage: experiments in pursuit of long life yielded a means of shortening it. This is not rhetorical irony but the ordinary condition of technical history—the road from purpose to consequence is never straight.*
