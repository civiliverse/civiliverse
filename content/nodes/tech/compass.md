**卷首引文** — 「方家以磁石磨针锋，则能指南，然常微偏东，不全南也。」——沈括《梦溪笔谈》（约1088）
*"Magicians rub a needle-point with lodestone, and then it can point south; yet it always inclines slightly east, not due south." — Shen Kuo, c. 1088*

**定位句** — 一根指南的针，最要紧的一句记载却是说它「指得不太准」。
*A needle that points south; the most consequential thing ever written about it is that it points slightly wrong.*

**历史叙述** — 战国典籍中的「司南」常被说成最早的磁罗盘，但至今无出土实物，1940年代王振铎的勺形复原属于推测，学界多持保留。可靠的记载自北宋始。《武经总要》（1044）述「指南鱼」：剪薄铁叶为鱼形，烧红后蘸水，令其沿地磁方向冷却而获磁性，浮于水面即可指向。约1088年，沈括在《梦溪笔谈》中比较了四种安置磁针的方法——水浮、置指甲、置碗唇、缕悬——判定缕悬最善；同一段里，他写下针「常微偏东」，这是世界上关于磁偏角的最早记录，比欧洲早约四百年。1119年，朱彧记广州蕃舶「夜则观星，昼则观日，阴晦观指南针」，磁针至此已是远洋航行的常规仪器。12世纪末，英格兰的内卡姆与法国的居约·德·普罗万各自提到浮针；此后地中海世界发展出装有方位盘的旱罗盘，并随大航海传布全球。
*The "south-pointing spoon" of Warring-States texts is often called the first magnetic compass, but no specimen has been excavated; Wang Zhenduo's spoon-shaped reconstruction of the 1940s is conjecture, and scholars largely reserve judgement. Reliable records begin with the Northern Song. The Wujing Zongyao (1044) describes a "south-pointing fish": a thin iron leaf cut to shape, heated red and quenched so that it cools along the earth's field and acquires magnetism, then floated on water. Around 1088 Shen Kuo compared four mountings for a magnetized needle—floated, balanced on a fingernail, on a bowl's rim, and hung by silk—and judged silk suspension best; in the same passage he noted that the needle points "slightly east," the world's earliest record of magnetic declination, some four centuries before Europe. In 1119 Zhu Yu wrote that on the foreign ships of Guangzhou the pilots "watch the stars by night, the sun by day, and in murk the south-pointing needle": the compass was by then standard equipment for the open sea. By the late twelfth century Alexander Neckam in England and Guyot de Provins in France mention a floating needle; the Mediterranean world went on to build the dry compass with an attached wind-rose, which sailed with the age of exploration to the rest of the world.*

**史论** — 沈括那半句「常微偏东」值得单独端详。指南针的用处是给出方向，而他记下的却是它**偏离**的量——这是把一件工具当作研究对象来看待的眼光，是仪器史上一次安静的分水岭。中国的记载止步于现象，欧洲直到16世纪吉尔伯特才把地球本身当成一块磁石来解释它，这一步的迟早与制度、问题意识有关，与聪明才智无关。另一层则关乎「谁在用」：磁针在中国最早的用途之一是堪舆择向，进入海船只是众多用法中的一支；而在地中海，它几乎一上手就绑在了航海图与方位盘上。同一件器物，在不同的社会里生长出不同的器官——技术从来不是自己决定自己命运的。
*Shen Kuo's half-sentence about the slight eastward inclination deserves separate attention. A compass exists to give direction, yet what he recorded was the amount by which it fails—the outlook of someone treating a tool as an object of study, a quiet watershed in the history of instruments. Chinese accounts stopped at the phenomenon; not until Gilbert in the sixteenth century did anyone explain it by treating the earth itself as a lodestone, and the timing of that step has to do with institutions and with what questions were worth asking, not with cleverness. A second layer concerns users: in China one of the needle's earliest applications was siting buildings and graves, and going to sea was only one branch among many, whereas in the Mediterranean it was bound almost immediately to the chart and the wind-rose. The same object grows different organs in different societies—technology never decides its own fate.*
