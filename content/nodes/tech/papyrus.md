**卷首引文** — 「我们的文明——至少我们的记载——极大地依赖于纸草的使用。」——老普林尼《自然史》卷十三
*"…our civilization—or at any rate our records—depend very largely on the use of papyrus." — Pliny the Elder, Natural History 13*

**定位句** — 尼罗河边的一种草，撑起了地中海世界三千年的书写。
*A Nile sedge that carried three thousand years of Mediterranean writing.*

**历史叙述** — 纸草取自尼罗河三角洲盛产的纸莎草。工匠剖取其茎髓为薄条，纵横交叠、压榨捶打，靠自身汁液黏合成张，再拼粘为长卷。自古埃及起，纸草便是书写主料，行销整个地中海：希腊的哲学、罗马的法令、亚历山大图书馆的十万卷藏书，大多写在纸草上。埃及几近垄断其产销，纸草因而成为一种带战略意味的出口物资。约公元前2世纪，珀加蒙据说因纸草禁运而改良羊皮纸（parchment）——书写材料的第一次「卡脖子」。此后羊皮纸在欧洲渐占上风，而8世纪以降，来自东方的纸又后来居上，纸草遂退出历史（塔阿利比记撒马尔罕纸「取代了埃及纸草」，见「造纸术」）。
*Papyrus was taken from the sedge that grew abundantly in the Nile Delta. Workers cut the pith into thin strips, laid them crosswise, pressed and beat them until their own sap bonded them into a sheet, then glued sheets into a long scroll. From ancient Egypt onward papyrus was the chief writing material, traded across the whole Mediterranean: Greek philosophy, Roman decrees, and the hundred thousand scrolls of the Library of Alexandria were mostly written on it. Egypt's near-monopoly made papyrus a strategic export. Around the 2nd century BCE, Pergamon is said to have improved parchment in response to a papyrus embargo—the first "supply squeeze" over a writing material. Parchment then gained ground in Europe, and from the 8th century paper from the East overtook both, and papyrus passed from history (al-Thaʿālibī records that Samarkand paper "drove out the papyrus of Egypt"; see "Papermaking").*

**史论** — 书写史常只记「写了什么」，却忘了问「写在哪」。纸草提醒我们：**载体也是技术**，而且载体的产地与供应，会左右知识的地理。埃及对纸草的近乎垄断，使地中海的读写在物质上系于一条尼罗河——珀加蒙被逼出羊皮纸的故事，是「技术自主」焦虑最古老的版本之一。纸草的终局也印证了「造纸术」里那句史论：材料会因地就材、代际更替，纸草让位于羊皮纸、再让位于纸，恰如接力。图谱把纸草（tech·书写材料）与字母（idea·书写原理）、图书馆（wonder·藏书之所）分层相连——一条 enables 边把它接入亚历山大图书馆：没有廉价的卷材，就没有十万卷的奇观。
*Histories of writing often record what was written but forget to ask what it was written on. Papyrus reminds us that the substrate is a technology too—and that where a substrate is made, and who supplies it, shapes the geography of knowledge. Egypt's near-monopoly tied Mediterranean literacy, materially, to a single river; the tale of Pergamon forced into parchment is one of the oldest versions of the anxiety over "technological self-reliance." Papyrus's end bears out the point made under "Papermaking": materials adapt to local supply and turn over by generations, papyrus yielding to parchment and then to paper, as in a relay. The graph layers papyrus (a tech, a writing material) with the alphabet (an idea, the principle of writing) and the Library (a wonder, a place of books)—an enables edge joins it to the Library of Alexandria: without a cheap scroll-stock, no wonder of a hundred thousand volumes.*
