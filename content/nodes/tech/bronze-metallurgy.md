**卷首引文** — 「金有六齐：六分其金而锡居一，谓之钟鼎之齐。」——《周礼·考工记》
*"There are six alloys of metal: with the metal in six parts and tin one, this is the alloy for bells and cauldrons." — Kaogongji, Rites of Zhou*

**定位句** — 人类第一次按配方造材料，而不只是捡到什么用什么。
*The first time humans made a material to a recipe, instead of using whatever they found.*

**历史叙述** — 铜在自然界可以天然生成，早期人类以冷锻加工。约公元前四千纪，西亚与安纳托利亚的工匠开始熔炼铜矿，并发现掺入砷可使器物变硬——砷铜由此出现，代价是冶炼者慢性中毒。约前3300年起，锡取代砷成为主要合金元素：锡青铜硬度高、熔点低于纯铜、流动性好，宜于铸造复杂器形。此后两千余年，青铜技术在欧亚多地展开——美索不达米亚与埃及的工具兵器、爱琴海的礼仪与贸易、中国二里头至商周的礼器铸造（以块范法制出饕餮纹方鼎，工艺路径与西亚失蜡法迥异）。锡的稀缺塑造了整个时代的地理：不列颠康沃尔、阿富汗、中亚的锡矿经数千公里贸易抵达各文明中心。约前1200年，东地中海诸国相继崩解，贸易网断裂，铜锡难继，而铁矿几乎处处皆有——铁器时代由此开端。
*Copper occurs in native form, and early humans worked it cold. In the fourth millennium BCE smiths in Southwest Asia and Anatolia began smelting copper ores and found that adding arsenic hardened the product—arsenical copper, at the cost of chronic poisoning for the smelter. From about 3300 BCE tin displaced arsenic: tin bronze is harder, melts below pure copper, and flows well, suiting complex castings. Over the following two millennia bronze technology unfolded across Eurasia—tools and weapons in Mesopotamia and Egypt, ritual and trade goods in the Aegean, and in China the ritual vessels of Erlitou through Shang and Zhou, whose piece-mould casting of taotie-decorated tetrapods followed a path quite distinct from West Asian lost-wax. Tin's scarcity shaped the geography of the age: ore from Cornwall, Afghanistan, and Central Asia traveled thousands of kilometres to the civilizational centres. Around 1200 BCE the states of the eastern Mediterranean fell one after another, the networks tore, copper and tin grew hard to obtain—and iron ore, which lies almost everywhere, took over.*

**史论** — 青铜的故事是一堂关于**供应链**的早课。它的优越不只在材料性能，更在于它逼出了一整套跨区域的组织：勘矿、开采、冶炼、长途运输、王室仓储与工匠世袭。也正因如此，它在体系崩塌时无从自救——一种依赖远方的技术，其命运握在自己够不着的地方。中国青铜的另一条支线同样值得注意：它的大宗用途不是农具而是礼器，铸鼎以定名分。这提示我们，同一材料在不同社会里回答的是不同的问题——把「有无青铜农具」当作先进与否的尺子，是拿一套问题去量另一套答案。
*Bronze is an early lesson in supply chains. Its advantage lay not only in material properties but in the organization it forced into being: prospecting, mining, smelting, long-haul transport, royal storehouses, hereditary craft. For the same reason it could not save itself when the system fell—a technology dependent on distance holds its fate somewhere it cannot reach. The Chinese branch is worth noting on its own terms: bronze there went overwhelmingly into ritual vessels rather than farm tools, and casting a cauldron was an act of political definition. The same material answers different questions in different societies; to measure advancement by the presence of bronze ploughshares is to bring one set of questions to another set of answers.*
