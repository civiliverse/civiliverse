**卷首引文** — 「麦罗埃，古代非洲的伯明翰。」——塞斯，1912年（其夸张已由后来的发掘修正）
*"Meroë, the Birmingham of ancient Africa." — A. H. Sayce, 1912; the exaggeration has since been corrected*

**定位句** — 一座被一句妙语捧起、又被一百年发掘放回原位的古城。
*A city raised up by one memorable phrase, and set back in place by a century of digging.*

**历史叙述** — 库施是尼罗河上游的古国，公元前8世纪曾北上统治埃及（第二十五王朝）。约前300年后，其政治中心南移至麦罗埃，历六百余年。城址周边散布着大片炉渣堆，考古学者由此确认了持续而可观的冶铁生产：块炼炉、鼓风管与铁器均有出土。1912年，塞斯以「古代非洲的伯明翰」形容此地，此语在此后一个世纪被反复引用——既被用作非洲古代技术成就的证据，也被用作反驳「非洲无历史」论的武器。近数十年的系统发掘则给出更审慎的图景：炉渣堆的体量虽大，但年代跨度长达数百年，年均产量远不及「工业城市」之喻；铁器在墓葬与遗址中的比例也说明它并未取代石、骨、铜的日常地位。与此同时，麦罗埃的其他成就得到确认：自有的麦罗埃文字（今可拼读而多不能解）、数以百计的陡峭金字塔、与罗马和印度洋世界的贸易。
*Kush was an ancient state of the upper Nile that ruled Egypt in the eighth century BCE as its Twenty-fifth Dynasty. After about 300 BCE its political centre moved south to Meroë, which lasted more than six centuries. Slag heaps spread around the site, and from them archaeologists confirmed sustained and substantial iron production: bloomery furnaces, tuyères, and iron objects have all been excavated. In 1912 Sayce called the place "the Birmingham of ancient Africa," and the phrase was quoted for the next hundred years—as evidence of African technical achievement and as a weapon against the claim that Africa had no history. Systematic excavation in recent decades gives a more careful picture: the heaps are large but accumulated over centuries, so annual output falls far short of the industrial metaphor, and the proportion of iron in graves and settlements shows it did not displace stone, bone, and copper in daily use. Meanwhile Meroë's other achievements stand confirmed: its own Meroitic script (today pronounceable but largely untranslatable), hundreds of steep-sided pyramids, and trade reaching Rome and the Indian Ocean world.*

**史论** — 这一条的教益，一半在麦罗埃，一半在「伯明翰」这个比喻。用工业革命的城市去命名一座古代非洲都城，本意是抬举，效果却是把评判尺度让给了对方：仿佛非洲的成就必须换算成欧洲的度量衡才算数。更稳妥的写法是让麦罗埃按自己的样子站着——它有自己的王统、文字、丧葬制度与冶金传统，这些不需要一个欧洲对应物来担保。本图谱把它单列节点，而不是作为「冶铁」的附庸，正是这个用意；同时把塞斯的修辞与后来的修正一并写出，是因为**史学的自我修正过程本身就是历史知识的一部分**，遮掉它，读者就只能在两种简化之间选一种。
*Half the lesson here concerns Meroë and half the metaphor. Naming an ancient African capital after a city of the Industrial Revolution was meant as praise, but its effect was to concede the measuring stick: as though African achievement counted only when converted into European units. The sounder course is to let Meroë stand as itself—it had its own dynasty, script, funerary system, and metallurgy, none of which require a European counterpart as guarantor. This graph gives it a node of its own rather than an appendage to "ironworking" for exactly that reason; and it prints both Sayce's phrase and its later correction, because the self-correcting work of historiography is itself part of historical knowledge. Conceal it, and the reader is left choosing between two simplifications.*
