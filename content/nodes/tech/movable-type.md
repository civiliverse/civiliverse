**卷首引文** — 「若止印三二本，未为简易；若印数十百千本，则极为神速。」——沈括《梦溪笔谈·技艺》（约1088）
*"For printing only two or three copies it would hardly be worth the trouble; but for tens, hundreds, or thousands, it is marvelously quick." — Shen Kuo, Dream Pool Essays, c. 1088*

**定位句** — 一项比古登堡早约四百年、在自己故乡却长期未成主流的发明。
*An invention that predated Gutenberg by some four centuries—yet never became the mainstream in its own homeland.*

**历史叙述** — 毕昇是北宋一介布衣，其事迹几乎全赖沈括《梦溪笔谈》数百字得以传世。约庆历年间（1041–1048），他以胶泥刻字，一字一印，薄如钱唇，火烧令坚。排版时于铁板上敷松脂、蜡与纸灰，置一铁范，密布字印，就火烘之，药熔则以平板压其面，使字平如砥；印毕再烘，字印可拆下重用。为求效率，常备两块铁板轮换：一板付印，一板已在布字，交替使用，"瞬息可就"。毕昇身后，其字印为沈括的侄辈所得。此法虽巧，然汉字动辄数千，需备字模以万计，且泥字不耐久；中国此后印刷仍以雕版为主流，活字要到王祯木活字（约1298）、明代铜活字，以及朝鲜半岛的金属活字（如1377年《直指》）方另辟蹊径。
*Bi Sheng was a commoner of the Northern Song whose deed survives almost solely through a few hundred characters in Shen Kuo's Dream Pool Essays. Around the 1040s he carved individual graphs in clay—each character its own piece, "thin as the rim of a coin"—and fired them hard. To compose a page he spread pine resin, wax, and paper-ash on an iron plate within an iron frame, packed it with type, warmed it until the binder softened, then pressed the faces flat "as a whetstone." After printing, reheating freed the type for reuse. He kept two plates in rotation—one printing while the next was being set—so the work went "in the blink of an eye." After his death his type passed to Shen Kuo's nephews. Ingenious as it was, Chinese needed thousands of graphs and tens of thousands of sorts, and clay type was perishable; woodblock printing remained China's mainstream. Movable type would find other paths only later—Wang Zhen's wooden type (c. 1298), Ming bronze type, and the metal type of the Korean peninsula (e.g. the Jikji, 1377).*

**史论** — 教科书惯以"毕昇早古登堡四百年"为荣，但这份"世界第一"需要一个更冷静的注脚。发明的先后与技术的普及是两回事：拼音文字几十个字母即可开印，汉字却要先囤起一座字库，"活字"的灵活在汉字面前先折了一半——技术上的先到，并不等于历史上的先赢。真正让活字改变世界的，从来不只是毕昇的泥，而是一整套社会条件（字母、纸、资本、竞争性市场），这些要到四百年后的欧洲才凑齐（见"印刷革命"）。因此活字印刷更宜作反辉格史的标本：同一发明在不同文明中的命运，取决于结构而非天才。毕昇与古登堡之间不宜画一支"影响"的箭头，而应以 parallels 无向边并置——各自独立，殊途而未必同归。
*Textbooks like to boast that Bi Sheng beat Gutenberg by four centuries, but this "world first" needs a cooler footnote. Being first to invent is not the same as being first to prevail: an alphabet opens shop with a few dozen letters, whereas Chinese must first stockpile a whole library of sorts, so half the flexibility of "movable" type is lost against the script itself. What made movable type world-changing was never the clay alone but a whole social apparatus—alphabet, paper, capital, competitive markets—that assembled in Europe only four centuries on (see "The Printing Revolution"). Movable type is thus best read as a specimen of anti-Whig history: the fate of one invention across civilizations turns on structure, not genius. Between Bi Sheng and Gutenberg we should draw no arrow of "influence," but set them side by side with an undirected parallels edge—independent origins that need not converge.*
