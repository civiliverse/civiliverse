**卷首引文** — 「撒马尔罕纸之精美，使其取代了此前所用的埃及纸草与皮纸。」——塔阿利比《奇趣珍闻之书》（约11世纪）
*"Among the specialities of Samarkand is paper, which has driven out the papyrus of Egypt and the parchment used before it." — al-Thaʿālibī, c. 11th c.*

**定位句** — 一种从中国出发、走了七百年才抵达欧洲的书写材料——没有它，古登堡无纸可印。
*A writing material that took seven centuries to travel from China to Europe—without it, Gutenberg would have had nothing to print on.*

**历史叙述** — 纸并非一人一日之功。西汉的放马滩、灞桥已有植物纤维纸的残片，说明「纸」在蔡伦之前数百年即已存在。东汉元兴元年（105年），尚方令蔡伦以树皮、麻头、破布、渔网为料改良其法，使纸廉价、匀薄、可量产，「自是莫不从用焉」。此后纸在东亚全面取代竹简缣帛。约8世纪，造纸术经中亚西传：撒马尔罕、巴格达（794年）相继设坊，以亚麻、棉布为料，纸自此进入伊斯兰世界的图书馆与官府；再经安达卢斯与意大利，于12—13世纪抵达拉丁欧洲。每一段旅程，纸都因地就材、改换纤维，却始终是同一种关于「让文字廉价」的技术。
*Paper was no single person's single day's work. Fibre-paper fragments from Western-Han Fangmatan and Baqiao show that "paper" existed centuries before Cai Lun. In 105 CE the workshop prefect Cai Lun improved the method with bark, hemp, rags, and fishing nets, making paper cheap, thin, and mass-producible, so that "from then on all adopted it." Paper then displaced bamboo and silk across East Asia. Around the 8th century the craft moved west: mills rose at Samarkand and at Baghdad (794), working flax and cotton rag, and paper entered the libraries and chanceries of the Islamic world; via al-Andalus and Italy it reached Latin Europe in the 12th–13th centuries. At each stage paper changed its fibre to suit local materials, yet remained the same technology for one purpose—making the written word cheap.*

**史论** — 把造纸术系于蔡伦一人，是英雄史观的老毛病；本图谱因此把「造纸术」（技术）与「蔡伦」（人物）分作两个节点——技术是物，人物是枢纽。真正值得端详的，是纸的**缓慢**：它花了整整七个世纪才走完从洛阳到美因茨的路。这提醒我们，技术传播从不是「先进自动扩散」，而要靠商路、战争、宗教与市场一节节接力。纸与印刷的相遇之所以在欧洲引爆、在东亚却未生同等剧变，答案同样不在纸本身，而在它落地时遇见的那套社会条件（见「印刷革命」）。
*To pin papermaking on Cai Lun alone is the old fault of the great-man view; this graph therefore splits "papermaking" (the technology) from "Cai Lun" (the person)—the technology is a thing, the person a hub. What repays attention is paper's slowness: it took a full seven centuries to travel from Luoyang to Mainz. Technology does not diffuse automatically because it is "advanced"; it is relayed, link by link, by trade routes, war, religion, and markets. That the meeting of paper and print detonated in Europe but not in East Asia turns, again, not on paper itself but on the social conditions it met on landing (see "The Printing Revolution").*
