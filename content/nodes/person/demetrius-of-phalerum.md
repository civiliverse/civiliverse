**卷首引文** — 「他受命征集天下之书，务求悉数汇于王之府库。」——《阿里斯特亚书信》（晚出材料）
*"He was charged to collect, if possible, all the books in the world." — the Letter of Aristeas (a late source)*

**定位句** — 一个可能提出了「把全世界的书都收起来」这个念头的人——可惜说这话的史料本身就不太可靠。
*A man who may have proposed collecting all the books in the world—except that the source saying so is not itself reliable.*

**历史叙述** — 德米特里出身法勒鲁姆，受学于亚里士多德的继承者泰奥弗拉斯托斯，属逍遥学派。前317年，马其顿的卡山德扶植他治理雅典，十年间他推行户口调查、限制奢靡葬仪、整顿法制，其统治毁誉参半；前307年德米特里一世·波利奥凯特斯攻入雅典，他出亡，辗转至埃及。托勒密一世在亚历山大城延揽学者、创设缪斯宫与图书馆，德米特里被认为参与其事。这一说法的主要来源是《阿里斯特亚书信》——一部讲述《七十士译本》缘起的文献，成书约在前2世纪，晚于德米特里一两个世纪，且旨在论证希腊文圣经译本的正统性，其叙事屡有夸饰。现代研究因此把他的角色定为「可能参与、程度不明」。晚期传记另载，托勒密一世死后他因曾支持另一位继承人而失宠，被逐后为毒蛇所噬而死；此类轶事同样出自晚出材料。
*Demetrius came from the deme of Phalerum and studied under Theophrastus, Aristotle's successor, in the Peripatetic school. In 317 BCE Cassander of Macedon installed him over Athens, and for ten years he conducted censuses, curbed extravagant funerals, and revised the laws, to mixed report; when Demetrius Poliorcetes took the city in 307 he fled, making his way in time to Egypt. Ptolemy I gathered scholars at Alexandria and founded the Museum and Library, and Demetrius is said to have taken part. The principal source for this is the Letter of Aristeas—an account of the origins of the Septuagint, composed around the second century BCE, a century or more after his death, and designed to establish the authority of the Greek Bible, with an embroidered narrative throughout. Modern scholarship therefore rates his role as possible participation of uncertain extent. Later biographical tradition adds that after Ptolemy I's death he lost favour for having backed a different successor, and died of a snakebite in exile; these anecdotes likewise come from late material.*

**史论** — 本条的存在理由，一半是图谱的结构需要（它是「谁想到要建图书馆」这条边的源端），一半是一个方法示范：**当史料不足时，正确的做法不是删掉这个人，也不是把传说写成事实，而是把不确定性一并建模。** 因此本条的 confidence 定为 low，指向图书馆的边标 disputed，正文明确交代《阿里斯特亚书信》的性质与年代。读者由此看到的不只是「德米特里参与创建了图书馆」，而是「有一条晚出材料这样说，而我们知道它为何可疑」。历史知识的诚实，恰在这两句话的差别里。
*This entry exists for two reasons: half structural, since it is the source end of the edge about who conceived the Library, and half as a methodological demonstration. When sources are thin, the right response is neither to delete the person nor to write legend as fact, but to model the uncertainty along with the claim. Hence a confidence of low, a disputed edge to the Library, and a plain statement in the body of what the Letter of Aristeas is and when it was written. What the reader sees is not "Demetrius helped found the Library" but "a late source says so, and we know why it is doubtful." The honesty of historical knowledge lies exactly in the difference between those two sentences.*
