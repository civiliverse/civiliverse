**卷首引文** — 「自古书契，多编以竹简……伦乃造意，用树肤、麻头及敝布、鱼网以为纸。」——《后汉书·宦者列传》
*"From antiquity writings were mostly strung on bamboo slips… Lun then conceived of making paper from tree bark, hemp ends, rags, and fishing nets." — Book of the Later Han, "Biographies of the Eunuchs"*

**定位句** — 被尊为"纸圣"的人，是造纸术得以推广的枢纽，也是一桩宫廷血案的参与者。
*The man revered as the "Paper Sage" was the hub through which papermaking spread—and a party to a palace killing.*

**历史叙述** — 蔡伦是东汉宦官，历任中常侍、尚方令，执掌皇家作坊。元兴元年（105年），他把以树皮、麻头、破布、渔网为原料改良的纸进献汉和帝，"帝善其能，自是莫不从用焉"，世称"蔡侯纸"；114年封龙亭侯。然而这位纸的功臣深陷宫廷政争：他早年曾奉命参与构陷安帝祖母宋贵人。邓太后崩后，安帝追究旧案，蔡伦自知难免，"乃沐浴整衣冠，饮药而死"（121年）。其生年史无明载，约在公元50至62年间。近数十年考古发现——放马滩、悬泉置乃至西汉灞桥的植物纤维残片——显示纸在蔡伦之前已存在；学界因而多认为他是造纸工艺的集大成改良者与推广者，而非无中生有的发明人。
*Cai Lun was a eunuch of the Eastern Han who rose to Regular Palace Attendant and Prefect of the Imperial Workshops. In 105 CE he presented to Emperor He a paper improved from tree bark, hemp ends, rags, and fishing nets; "the Emperor praised his ability, and from then on all adopted it," so it was called "Marquis Cai's paper." In 114 he was enfeoffed Marquis of Longting. Yet the benefactor of paper was enmeshed in court intrigue: years earlier he had been ordered to help frame Consort Song, grandmother of the future Emperor An. When the Empress Dowager Deng died and An reopened the case, Cai, knowing his fate, "bathed, dressed in formal robes, and drank poison" (121 CE). His birth year is unrecorded—somewhere between c. 50 and 62 CE. Excavations of recent decades—plant-fiber fragments from Fangmatan, Xuanquanzhi, and even Western-Han Baqiao—show paper existed before him; scholars therefore mostly regard him as the great improver and promoter of papermaking, not its inventor from nothing.*

**史论** — 民间尊蔡伦为"纸圣"，但把造纸术系于一人之名，恰是英雄史观的经典误置。蔡伦的历史位置不是"原因"，而是枢纽：皇家作坊的资源、宫廷的推广渠道、既有的抄纸经验，在他这里汇成一股合力，使一种更廉价的书写材料得以标准化、规模化、制度化。他更像一位卓越的"研发主管"，而非孤独的天才。他的结局也提醒我们，技术史从不悬浮于政治之外——同一双把纸推向天下的手，也曾伸进宫廷的血案。故其词条以 contributed（对造纸术）一类边接入图谱，而不是一条简单的"发明"箭头；皇家作坊的赞助关系俟「汉和帝／东汉尚方」节点建成后，以正确方向（赞助者→被赞助者）另挂 patronized 边。
*Folk tradition honors Cai Lun as the "Paper Sage," but pinning papermaking on one name is a classic misplacement of the great-man view. Cai's place in history is not "cause" but hub: the resources of the imperial workshops, the court's channels of promotion, and existing paper-making know-how converged in him, letting a cheaper writing material be standardized, scaled, and institutionalized. He resembles an outstanding "head of R&D" more than a lone genius. His end, too, reminds us that the history of technology never floats free of politics—the same hand that carried paper to the empire had reached into a palace killing. His entry therefore joins the graph through a contributed edge (to papermaking), not a single arrow of "invention"; the imperial-workshop patronage will be attached later, in the correct direction (patron→beneficiary), once a 'Emperor He / Han imperial workshops' node exists.*
