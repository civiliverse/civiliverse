**卷首引文** — 「他既为王，说谎于他便比旁人更为可耻，故其记述在我看来尤可凭信。」——阿里安《亚历山大远征记》序
*"Since he was a king, mendacity would have been more disgraceful for him—so I hold his account the more trustworthy." — Arrian, Anabasis, preface*

**定位句** — 一位用刀剑分得埃及、又用金库搜罗天下之书的将军——图书馆是他留下的另一种征服。
*A general who won Egypt by the sword and gathered the world's books with his treasury—the Library was his other kind of conquest.*

**历史叙述** — 托勒密（拉戈斯之子）是亚历山大最信任的将领之一。亚历山大前323年猝逝后，帝国裂于诸将，托勒密据埃及，前305年正式称王，号「索特尔」（救主），开创统治埃及近三百年的托勒密王朝。他在新都亚历山大城营建「缪斯宫」——一座供养学者的研究机构，及其附属图书馆；据《阿里斯特亚书信》等后世文献，此举或采法勒鲁姆的德米特里之议。搜书之法近乎国家工程：靠港船只所载书籍被搜出抄录，正本留馆、副本还船。托勒密本人也提笔为史，写下亲历的亚历山大远征，虽已散佚，却因阿里安的征引而部分留存——阿里安更以「王者不屑说谎」为由，视其为最可信的史源之一。
*Ptolemy son of Lagos was among Alexander's most trusted generals. When Alexander died suddenly in 323 BCE and the empire split among his commanders, Ptolemy took Egypt and in 305 declared himself king, styled "Soter" (Saviour), founding a dynasty that ruled Egypt for nearly three centuries. In his new capital of Alexandria he built the Mouseion—a research institution supporting scholars—and its Library; by later sources such as the Letter of Aristeas, perhaps on the advice of Demetrius of Phalerum. The method of collection was near-state: books aboard ships in harbour were seized and copied, the originals kept and copies returned. Ptolemy himself wrote history, an eyewitness account of Alexander's campaigns; though lost, it survives in part through Arrian's citations—and Arrian, on the ground that "a king would scorn to lie," ranked it among his most reliable sources.*

**史论** — 托勒密一世最适合诠释 patronized 这条边的方向：赞助者→被赞助者。图书馆不是「他发明的」，而是他用王权与金库**供养**起来的——真正的「原因」是这套把学问纳入国家开支的赞助制度，个人只是它的启动者与门面。这也点出奇观的双面性：搜罗天下之书的雄心，同时是征用天下之书的雄心（靠港船只的书被强制抄录即一例）。普世的知识理想，与帝国的占有欲，在这座图书馆里本就是同一枚硬币。故本节点以 patronized 边接入图书馆，而非画一支「他创造了知识」的箭头。
*Ptolemy I best illustrates the direction of the patronized edge: patron→beneficiary. The Library was not "his invention" but something he sustained with royal power and treasury—the real "cause" was the patronage system that folded scholarship into state expenditure, the man merely its initiator and figurehead. This also marks the double face of a wonder: the ambition to gather the world's books was also the ambition to requisition them (ships in harbour had their books seized and copied). The universal ideal of knowledge and the imperial appetite for possession were, in this Library, two sides of one coin. The node therefore joins the Library through a patronized edge, not an arrow of "he created knowledge."*
