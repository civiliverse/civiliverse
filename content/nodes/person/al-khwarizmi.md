**卷首引文** — 「我作此书，取算术中最易且最有用者——人们在继承、遗产、分产、诉讼与贸易中时时需用之事。」——花拉子米《代数学》序（约820年）
*"I have composed… what is easiest and most useful in arithmetic, such as men constantly require in cases of inheritance, legacies, partition, lawsuits, and trade." — al-Khwārizmī, preface to the Algebra*

**定位句** — 他的名字变成了「算法」，他的书名变成了「代数」——很少有人的姓氏，被人类日日念叨。
*His name became "algorithm," his book's title became "algebra"—few surnames are invoked by humanity so daily.*

**历史叙述** — 花拉子米约780年生，活跃于阿拔斯王朝首都巴格达的学术圈（后世常称「智慧宫」，见 #17），治数学、天文与地理。约820年，他献给哈里发马蒙一部《还原与对消之书》(Kitāb al-jabr wa'l-muqābala)：书名中的 al-jabr（「还原」，指移项使方程复原）即「代数」algebra 的词源。此书系统处理一次、二次方程的求解，以文字（非符号）陈述，却把「代数」立成一门独立学问。他另著《印度数字算法》，向阿拉伯世界介绍含零的十进位数码；此书拉丁译本开篇 *Dixit Algoritmi*（「花拉子米说」），其名 Algoritmi 遂演成「算法」(algorithm) 一词。他还编订天文表、参与测地、绘制《大地形胜》。
*Al-Khwārizmī was born around 780 and worked in the scholarly circle of Abbasid Baghdad (later often called the House of Wisdom; see #17), across mathematics, astronomy, and geography. Around 820 he presented to the caliph al-Ma'mun a Book of Restoration and Balancing (Kitāb al-jabr wa'l-muqābala): the al-jabr in its title ("restoration," the moving of terms to restore an equation) is the root of the word "algebra." The book treated the solution of linear and quadratic equations, stated in words rather than symbols, yet established algebra as a discipline in its own right. He also wrote On the Hindu Art of Reckoning, introducing the decimal numerals with zero to the Arab world; the Latin version opens Dixit Algoritmi ("al-Khwārizmī said"), and Algoritmi became our "algorithm." He compiled astronomical tables, took part in geodesy, and mapped the known world.*

**史论** — 花拉子米又一次印证了本图谱的编例：**人物是枢纽，不是唯一的原因**。他没有「独自发明」代数（希腊丢番图、印度婆罗摩笈多、巴比伦算题各有贡献），也没有发明印度数字——他的历史位置在**综合与转手**：把印度的数码、希腊的几何、巴比伦的算术，熔进阿拉伯文的著作，再传向东西两方。而那句被反复引用的自序透露了另一层：他明说，这套高深数学是为**继承、分产、诉讼、贸易**这些俗务服务的——数学的根扎在市集与遗产纠纷里，不在象牙塔。「代数」「算法」两个词至今天天被念，恰是传播史留在语言里的活化石，与「阿拉伯数字」的错名（见 #15）同理：名字记住的，往往是知识走过的路。图谱以 contributed 边让他连向「印度—阿拉伯数字」「代数」与「智慧宫」。
*Al-Khwārizmī again bears out this graph's editorial rule: a person is a hub, not the sole cause. He did not "invent algebra alone" (Diophantus in Greece, Brahmagupta in India, and Babylonian problem-texts all contributed), nor did he invent the Indian numerals—his place in history lies in synthesis and relay: folding Indian digits, Greek geometry, and Babylonian arithmetic into Arabic works and passing them east and west. His much-quoted preface reveals another layer: he says plainly that this advanced mathematics serves inheritance, partition, lawsuits, and trade—mathematics rooted in the marketplace and the estate dispute, not the ivory tower. That "algebra" and "algorithm" are spoken daily still is a living fossil of transmission left in language, of a piece with the misnomer "Arabic numerals" (see #15): what a name remembers is usually the road knowledge travelled. The graph joins him by contributed edges to "Hindu–Arabic numerals," "algebra," and the "House of Wisdom."*
