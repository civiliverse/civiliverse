**卷首引文** — 「此项技艺首创于美因茨……始于主历1440年。」——《科隆编年史》（1499）
*"This highly worthy art was first invented at Mainz… and it was begun in the year of our Lord 1440." — The Cologne Chronicle (1499)*

**定位句** — 印刷术的「发明家」，却在自己的官司里输掉了印刷厂——发明与获利，从来是两码事。
*The "inventor" of printing who lost his print shop in his own lawsuit—invention and profit were never the same thing.*

**历史叙述** — 关于古登堡本人，留存的确凿史料出奇地少。他约1400年生于美因茨一个市民家庭，一度旅居斯特拉斯堡，从事金工与镜片生意，其间似已试制印刷。约1450年，他在美因茨向富商约翰·福斯特借得大笔资金，建起印坊，印出四十二行圣经等。然而1455年，福斯特就债务对他提起诉讼（记录见《赫尔马斯佩格公证书》），古登堡败诉，印坊、设备连同尚未售完的圣经一并判归福斯特——福斯特遂与其女婿、能干的彼得·舍费尔续办印业，声名与利润多归了他们。古登堡晚年潦倒，1465年获美因茨大主教一个荣衔性的闲职以糊口，1468年去世，身后声名迟至数十年后才由《科隆编年史》等追认。
*Firm sources on Gutenberg himself are surprisingly scarce. He was born around 1400 to a Mainz burgher family, lived for a time in Strasbourg working in metal and mirrors, and there seems already to have experimented with printing. Around 1450, back in Mainz, he borrowed heavily from the merchant Johann Fust, set up a workshop, and printed the 42-line Bible and other works. But in 1455 Fust sued him over the debt (recorded in the Helmasperger notarial instrument); Gutenberg lost, and his workshop, equipment, and unsold Bibles passed to Fust—who, with his able son-in-law Peter Schöffer, carried on the business, taking most of the fame and profit. Gutenberg ended his days poor, granted a nominal sinecure by the Archbishop of Mainz in 1465 to live on, and died in 1468; his renown was affirmed only decades later, by sources such as the Cologne Chronicle.*

**史论** — 古登堡是「人物是枢纽、不是原因」这条编例的又一注脚，且带着一层辛辣：欧洲印刷术的「发明家」，本人几乎没从这项改变世界的技术里获益——他先输了官司，再输了署名（最早的印本多不署他名）。这提醒我们两件事。其一，「谁发明了印刷术」远比教科书说的复杂：金属铸字、油墨、压印机各有来路，古登堡的功绩在**集成**而非凭空创造。其二，技术的历史从不等于发明者的传记——真正让印刷「革命」的，是它落地的那套社会条件（见「印刷革命」），而非古登堡一人的天才。图谱因此让他以 contributed 边连向「古登堡印刷机」，把「革命」留给社会去承担。
*Gutenberg is another footnote to the rule that "a person is a hub, not a cause," and a sharp one: the "inventor" of European printing scarcely profited from the world-changing technology—he lost first the lawsuit, then the credit (the earliest printed books mostly bear other names). This teaches two things. First, "who invented printing" is far more tangled than textbooks say: metal casting, ink, and the press each had their own origins, and Gutenberg's achievement was integration, not creation from nothing. Second, the history of a technology is never the biography of its inventor—what made printing "revolutionary" was the social conditions it met (see "The Printing Revolution"), not one man's genius. The graph therefore joins him to "the Gutenberg Press" by a contributed edge, leaving the "revolution" to be borne by society.*
