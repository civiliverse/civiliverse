**卷首引文** — 「五日之内，伦敦的死亡人数较常年同期多出约四千。」——英国卫生部报告（1954）
*"In the space of five days, deaths in London exceeded the seasonal norm by about four thousand." — UK Ministry of Health, 1954*

**定位句** — 一场天气与一座城市的燃料习惯相遇，五天里杀死的人比多数战役都多。
*A spell of weather met a city's fuel habits, and five days killed more people than most battles.*

**历史叙述** — 伦敦的煤烟污染由来已久，中世纪已有禁烧海煤的诏令，19世纪的「豌豆汤浓雾」更成为城市的标记。1952年12月5日，一个反气旋停驻于泰晤士河谷上空，风止，冷空气被上方的暖层压住，形成逆温。城中数百万户家庭燃烧的劣质煤（战后优质煤多用于出口）、燃煤电厂与柴油公交的排放无处扩散，二氧化硫与烟尘在近地面积聚，与水汽结合成酸性气溶胶。能见度降至数米，公交须有人持灯步行引导，救护车停驶，萨德勒之井剧院因观众看不见舞台而中止演出，牲畜在史密斯菲尔德牛市窒息倒毙。四日后西风到来，烟雾散去。医院的接诊记录与殡仪馆的排队随即揭示了代价：呼吸道与心血管疾病死亡陡增，其中老人与既有病患最重。政府起初倾向于归咎流感，压力之下终于设立比弗委员会，1956年《清洁空气法》确立无烟区、补贴改用无烟燃料并推动电厂迁出市区。
*London's coal smoke was an old affliction: medieval decrees against burning sea-coal, and by the nineteenth century the "pea-souper" as a mark of the city. On 5 December 1952 an anticyclone settled over the Thames valley; the wind dropped and a layer of warm air capped the cold, forming an inversion. The poor-grade coal burned in millions of households (the better grades went for export after the war), together with coal-fired power stations and diesel buses, had nowhere to disperse, and sulphur dioxide and particulates accumulated near the ground, combining with moisture into an acid aerosol. Visibility fell to a few metres; buses were led by men on foot with lamps, ambulances stopped running, Sadler's Wells halted a performance because the audience could not see the stage, and cattle suffocated at the Smithfield market. Four days later a westerly arrived and the smog cleared. Hospital admissions and the queues at undertakers then revealed the cost: sharp excess mortality from respiratory and cardiovascular disease, heaviest among the old and already ill. The government at first inclined to blame influenza; under pressure it appointed the Beaver Committee, and the Clean Air Act of 1956 established smokeless zones, subsidized the conversion to smokeless fuel, and pushed power stations out of the city.*

**史论** — 大烟雾的教训不在于「污染有害」——这一点从中世纪的禁令到19世纪的讽刺画都已说过无数遍——而在于**何以知道了仍不行动**。伦敦人与煤烟共处了几个世纪，浓雾被写进小说、画进版画、当作城市性格的一部分；正是这种熟悉，使危险被归入「天气」而非「排放」。真正改变政策的，是把死亡数字与那五天严丝合缝地对上的统计工作：当伤害第一次被计量为「超额死亡」，它才从一种氛围变成一件事实。这与二十年后《寂静的春天》所做的事同构——从看得见的不适，到可归因的损害，中间隔着一套方法。本图谱把大烟雾与 DDT 生态危机同接入环保运动，正是要显示：现代环境治理不是由某一次觉醒开启的，而是由一连串各自计量出来的灾难累积推动的。
*The lesson of the Great Smog is not that pollution harms—said countless times from medieval decrees to nineteenth-century cartoons—but why knowing was not acting. Londoners had lived with coal smoke for centuries; the fog was written into novels, etched into prints, treated as part of the city's character. That very familiarity filed the danger under weather rather than emissions. What changed policy was the statistical work that matched the death toll precisely to those five days: only when harm was first counted as excess mortality did it turn from an atmosphere into a fact. This is structurally what Silent Spring did twenty years later—between visible discomfort and attributable damage lies a method. The graph runs both the Great Smog and the DDT ecological crisis into the environmental movement to show that modern environmental governance was not opened by a single awakening, but pushed by an accumulation of separately measured disasters.*
