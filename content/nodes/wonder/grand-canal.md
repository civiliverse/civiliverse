**卷首引文** — 「尽道隋亡为此河，至今千里赖通波。」——皮日休《汴河怀古》
*"All say the Sui fell because of this canal—yet to this day a thousand li rely on its flowing waves." — Pi Rixiu, "Reflecting on the Bian Canal"*

**定位句** — 一条把中国南北缝在一起的人工河——它拖垮了修它的王朝，却养活了此后一千年的帝国。
*An artificial river that stitched north and south China together—it broke the dynasty that dug it, yet fed the empire for a thousand years after.*

**历史叙述** — 中国的经济重心自魏晋以降渐移江南，而政治军事中心长在北方，南粮北运遂成帝国命脉。大运河正是为此而生。它并非一朝一夕之功：春秋的邗沟、诸代的旧渠早已零星存在，隋炀帝大业年间（605–609）征发百余万民夫，把这些河段连缀、开浚为一条贯通南北的大动脉，工程浩大而民力惨重，成为隋速亡的口实之一。唐宋赖漕运而兴，汴河「岁漕江淮米数百万石」，运河两岸城市繁盛。元代定都大都（北京），将运河裁弯取直，改为京杭直连，全程约1800公里。此后明清续赖其运漕。它是前工业时代世界最长的人工水道，部分河段至今通航，2014年列入世界遗产。
*China's economic center of gravity shifted southward to the Yangtze from the Wei-Jin period on, while the political and military center remained in the north—so moving southern grain north became the empire's lifeline. The Grand Canal was made for this. It was no work of a single reign: the Han Gou of the Spring and Autumn era and canals of many dynasties already existed in pieces; in the Daye years (605–609) Emperor Yang of Sui conscripted over a million labourers to link and dredge these into one continuous artery north to south—a vast work at cruel human cost, and one pretext blamed for the Sui's swift fall. Tang and Song rose on this grain transport, the Bian Canal carrying "several million shi of Yangtze-Huai rice a year," with prosperous cities along its banks. The Yuan, capital now at Dadu (Beijing), straightened the canal into a direct Beijing–Hangzhou line of some 1,800 km. Ming and Qing relied on it still. It was the longest artificial waterway of the pre-industrial world, parts navigable today, and a World Heritage Site since 2014.*

**史论** — 大运河是「巨型工程」双面性的绝佳标本，皮日休那两句诗已把张力道尽：世人都说隋朝亡于修河，可千里漕运至今仍赖它——一项工程可以既是暴政的罪证，又是文明的血脉。图谱把它列为 wonder（土木奇观），但奇观的史论必须**同时写下代价**：贯通运河的，是百余万民夫的劳役与伤亡，这一笔账不容一句「伟大工程」轻轻抹去（叙述据实、不煽情）。更深一层，大运河提醒我们「基础设施」在科技史中的分量：它本身不产知识，却把南北的粮食、货物、士人、书籍连成一张流通之网——宋代繁荣的书籍市场与商业文明，正是在这张网上运转。故图谱以一条 disputed 的 enables 边，把大运河谨慎地接向「雕版印刷」所依托的流通条件：运河不是印刷的原因，却是它得以铺开的地利之一。
*The Grand Canal is a superb specimen of the double face of the megaproject, and Pi Rixiu's couplet says it all: everyone blames the Sui's fall on digging the canal, yet a thousand li of grain transport rely on it still—one work can be at once the evidence of tyranny and the bloodstream of a civilization. The graph lists it as a wonder (of civil engineering), but a wonder's historical gaze must write the cost as well: the canal was joined by the forced labour, and deaths, of over a million conscripts, an account that no phrase like "great engineering" may lightly erase (stated plainly, without sentiment). Deeper still, the Grand Canal shows the weight of "infrastructure" in the history of technology: it produced no knowledge itself, yet wove the grain, goods, scholars, and books of north and south into one circulating network—on which the Song's thriving book market and commercial civilization ran. The graph therefore joins the canal, cautiously and through a disputed enables edge, to the circulatory conditions on which "woodblock printing" depended: the canal was not the cause of print, but one of the favourable grounds on which it could spread.*
