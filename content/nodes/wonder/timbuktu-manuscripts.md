**卷首引文** — 「盐来自北方，金来自南方，而神的话语与智慧的珍宝，来自廷巴克图。」——西非流传谚语（约16世纪）
*"Salt comes from the north, gold from the south, but the word of God and the treasures of wisdom come from Timbuktu." — West African proverb, c. 16th c.*

**定位句** — 撒哈拉以南非洲从不是「没有历史的大陆」——廷巴克图的家族书架上，躺着几十万册手稿。
*Sub-Saharan Africa was never "a continent without history"—hundreds of thousands of manuscripts still rest on Timbuktu's family shelves.*

**历史叙述** — 廷巴克图位于撒哈拉南缘、尼日尔河曲，13世纪起因跨撒哈拉的盐—金—奴隶贸易而富，成为马里、桑海帝国的学术与宗教中心。以桑科雷清真寺为核心的学府群吸引各地学者，讲授《古兰》学、法学、逻辑、天文、数学与医学；学者阿赫马德·巴巴等著述宏富。此地既抄进从北非、埃及、安达卢斯流来的阿拉伯典籍，也用阿拉伯字母拼写本地语言（阿贾米文）著书。数以万计的手稿世代藏于家族与私人图书馆，至今犹存。2012年，圣战武装占领廷巴克图、威胁焚毁手稿，当地图书馆员与民众冒险将约35万册秘密转运至巴马科，护住了这份记忆。
*Timbuktu stands on the southern edge of the Sahara at the bend of the Niger. From the 13th century it grew rich on the trans-Saharan trade in salt, gold, and slaves, becoming a scholarly and religious center of the Mali and Songhai empires. Colleges centered on the Sankore mosque drew scholars from afar to teach Quranic science, law, logic, astronomy, mathematics, and medicine; savants such as Ahmad Baba wrote prolifically. The city copied in Arabic texts flowing from North Africa, Egypt, and al-Andalus, and also wrote in local languages set down in Arabic script (Ajami). Tens of thousands of manuscripts, held for generations in family and private libraries, survive to this day. In 2012, when jihadist fighters occupied Timbuktu and threatened to burn the manuscripts, local librarians and residents secretly moved some 350,000 volumes to Bamako at great risk, saving this memory.*

**史论** — 廷巴克图手稿群的史论价值，在于它正面驳倒一种曾被冠冕堂皇说出的偏见——黑格尔说非洲「没有历史」，特雷弗-罗珀说非洲史不过是「野蛮人在无关紧要的角落打转」。几十万册以本地之手写就的天文、数学、法学手稿，是对这类论断最平静也最有力的反证。这是一个「反辉格」的关键节点：文明、识字、科学从来不是某一大洲的专利。图谱把它列入「知识奇观」群，与亚历山大图书馆、那烂陀、智慧宫以 parallels 无向边相连——提醒读者，人类的求知之光，在撒哈拉以南同样长燃。而2012年的抢救更给这份「奇观」添了一重当下的分量：知识的存续，从不是理所当然，往往靠具体的人在危难中一册册背出来。（此处涉威胁与抢救，叙述据实、不渲染。）
*The Timbuktu manuscripts matter historiographically because they refute, head-on, a prejudice once uttered with full pomp—Hegel's claim that Africa had "no history," Trevor-Roper's that African history was but "the unrewarding gyrations of barbarous tribes." Hundreds of thousands of manuscripts on astronomy, mathematics, and law, written by local hands, are the calmest and strongest rebuttal to such verdicts. This is a key anti-Whig node: civilization, literacy, and science were never any one continent's monopoly. The graph places it in the cluster of "knowledge wonders," joined by undirected parallels edges to the Library of Alexandria, Nalanda, and the House of Wisdom—reminding the reader that the light of human inquiry burned in sub-Saharan Africa too. The 2012 rescue gives this "wonder" a further present-day weight: the survival of knowledge is never guaranteed, and often depends on specific people carrying it out, volume by volume, through danger. (The threat and rescue are stated plainly, without sensational colour.)*
