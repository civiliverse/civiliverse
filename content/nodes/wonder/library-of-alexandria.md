**卷首引文** — 「法勒鲁姆的德米特里，王家图书馆之长，领受巨资，以期尽其所能，搜罗天下之书。」——《阿里斯特亚书信》§9（伪托，约公元前2世纪）
*"Demetrius of Phalerum, the president of the king's library, received vast sums of money, for the purpose of collecting together, as far as he possibly could, all the books in the world." — Letter of Aristeas §9 (pseudepigraphic, c. 2nd c. BCE)*

**定位句** — 一座知识的国家工程，和一则关于它如何"毁于一炬"的、并不真实的传说。
*A state enterprise of knowledge—and a not-quite-true legend of how it "burned in a single night."*

**历史叙述** — 亚历山大图书馆约公元前3世纪初建于托勒密王朝治下的亚历山大城，是"缪斯宫"（Mouseion，"博物馆"museum 的词源）的一部分。构想或始于托勒密一世（索特尔），据传得法勒鲁姆的德米特里献策；实体建成与扩充多在托勒密二世（费拉德尔福斯）之世。它以近乎国家工程的规模搜罗希腊语世界的抄本：靠港船只所载书籍被搜出抄录，正本留馆、副本还船。藏书数难以确证，古代文献有二十万乃至五十万卷之说，今人估计约四万至四十万卷不等。这里供养逾百名学者，衣食、住所与免税俱备；欧几里得、埃拉托斯特尼、萨莫色雷斯的阿里斯塔克斯（曾任馆长）等曾在此工作，文献编目学（卡利马科斯的《书目》Pinakes）亦于此奠基。
*The Library of Alexandria was founded in the early 3rd century BCE under the Ptolemies as part of the Mouseion (the "institution of the Muses," root of the word museum). The idea may have begun with Ptolemy I Soter, reportedly on the advice of Demetrius of Phalerum; the physical institution was built and expanded mainly under Ptolemy II Philadelphus. It gathered the manuscripts of the Greek-speaking world on a near-state scale: books aboard ships in harbor were seized and copied, the originals kept and copies returned. Its holdings cannot be verified—ancient sources speak of 200,000 or even 500,000 scrolls, modern estimates range from 40,000 to 400,000. It supported over a hundred scholars with food, lodging, and tax exemption; Euclid, Eratosthenes, and Aristarchus of Samothrace (a head librarian) worked here, and the science of cataloguing (Callimachus's Pinakes) was founded here.*

**史论** — 关于它的"焚毁"，流传最广的是一场惊天大火——凯撒、基督徒或穆斯林，取决于讲故事的人想责怪谁。但史学的共识远不那么戏剧化：图书馆是在数百年间慢慢凋零的。公元前145年托勒密八世驱逐学者、前48年凯撒战火殃及港区书栈、公元272年奥勒良夷平王宫区、391年子馆塞拉皮雍被毁——每一击都真实，却没有哪一击是那"一场大火"。把损失归于单一暴行，既满足了"黄金时代毁于野蛮"的想象，也免除了对"制度性衰败"的追问。作为奇观，它的代价同样值得直视：这座"人类知识的圣殿"，其书架有一部分是靠王权强制征收填满的（靠港船只的书被搜出抄录即一例）——普世的雄心，也是征用的雄心。
*Of its "destruction," the most popular tale is one great fire—by Caesar, by Christians, or by Muslims, depending on whom the teller wishes to blame. But the scholarly consensus is far less dramatic: the Library withered slowly across centuries. Ptolemy VIII's expulsion of scholars in 145 BCE, Caesar's fire reaching the dockside stores in 48 BCE, Aurelian's razing of the palace quarter in 272 CE, the destruction of the daughter library at the Serapeum in 391 CE—each blow was real, yet none was "the" fire. To lay the loss on a single act of barbarism both satisfies the fantasy of a golden age destroyed by savages and excuses us from asking about institutional decay. As a wonder, its costs deserve a level gaze too: this "temple of human knowledge" filled part of its shelves by royal confiscation (ships in harbor had their books seized and copied)—an ambition to gather the universal that was also an ambition to requisition.*
