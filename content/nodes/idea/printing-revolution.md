**卷首引文** — 「印刷术、火药与磁石这三者，改变了全世界事物的整个面貌与状态：其一在于文事，其二在于兵事，其三在于航海。」——培根《新工具》第一卷·箴言129（1620）
*"…printing, gunpowder, and the magnet. For these three have changed the whole face and state of things throughout the world; the first in literature, the second in warfare, the third in navigation…" — Francis Bacon, Novum Organum, I.129 (1620)*

**定位句** — 让知识第一次可以被精确复制的那场变革——以及关于它是否真那么"革命"的一场争论。
*The change that first let knowledge be copied exactly—and the argument over whether it was really so "revolutionary."*

**历史叙述** — 约1440年代，美因茨的金匠约翰内斯·古登堡把金属活字、油性墨与改自榨葡萄、压纸的螺旋压印机组合起来，约1455年印出四十二行圣经。与东亚的泥、木活字不同，欧洲的拼音文字只需几十种字模，天然适配活字量产。此后半个世纪，印刷术以惊人速度扩散：到1500年，欧洲已有约二百八十座城镇设有印坊，所印"摇篮本"（incunabula）版本数以万计、册数以百万计。书籍由抄工的奢侈品变为可批量复制的商品；标准化的文本、图表与页码，使知识得以精确征引、跨地校勘、长期保存。宗教改革的传单、人文主义的古典校勘本、天文与解剖的精印图谱，皆乘此东风。
*In the 1440s Johannes Gutenberg, a goldsmith of Mainz, combined metal type, oil-based ink, and a screw press adapted from the wine-and-paper press, and around 1455 printed the 42-line Bible. Unlike the clay and wooden type of East Asia, Europe's alphabetic script needed only a few dozen sorts and suited mass composition. Over the next half-century printing spread astonishingly fast: by 1500, presses operated in some 280 European towns, and the "incunabula" they produced ran to tens of thousands of editions and millions of copies. Books turned from a scribe's luxury into a commodity one could reproduce at scale; standardized texts, diagrams, and page numbers let knowledge be cited precisely, collated across distances, and preserved. The pamphlets of the Reformation, the humanist editions of the classics, the finely printed atlases of astronomy and anatomy—all rode this wind.*

**史论** — 伊丽莎白·爱森斯坦在《作为变革动因的印刷机》（1979）中论证，印刷带来的"固定性"（fixity）、标准化与广泛传播，是宗教改革与科学革命的隐形引擎。但把一切归功于机器是危险的：东亚早有活字却未掀起同等波澜，可见"革命"的主角并非压印机本身，而是与之相遇的字母、纸张、商业资本与竞争性市场。阿德里安·约翰斯（《书籍的性质》，1998）更反驳道，"固定性"并非印刷的天赋，而是几百年间靠制度与信任一点点造出来的——早期印本盗版丛生、错讹遍地。何况印刷并不只照亮世界：它同样高效地印出了禁书目录、宣传单与被标准化的谬误。故本节点宜与"活字印刷"以 parallels 并置，提醒读者：革命属于社会，而不属于某一台机器。
*In The Printing Press as an Agent of Change (1979), Elizabeth Eisenstein argued that print's "fixity," standardization, and wide dissemination were hidden engines of the Reformation and the Scientific Revolution. But crediting the machine with everything is risky: East Asia had movable type without a comparable upheaval, which shows the protagonist of the "revolution" was not the press but the alphabet, paper, commercial capital, and competitive markets it met. Adrian Johns (The Nature of the Book, 1998) went further, countering that fixity was no innate gift of print but something built up over centuries through institutions and trust—early print teemed with piracy and error. And printing did not only illuminate: it printed indexes of forbidden books, propaganda sheets, and standardized falsehoods just as efficiently. This node therefore belongs beside "Movable-Type Printing" under a parallels edge, a reminder that revolutions belong to societies, not to any single machine.*
