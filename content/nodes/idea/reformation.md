**卷首引文** — 「教皇要么废除知识与印刷，要么印刷终将把他连根拔起。」——约翰·福克斯《殉道者名录》(1563)
*"Either the pope must abolish knowledge and printing, or printing must at length root him out." — John Foxe, Acts and Monuments (1563)*

**定位句** — 第一场「媒介革命」的政治后果——路德的小册子，是印刷术印出的第一批「畅销书」。
*The political fallout of the first "media revolution"—Luther's pamphlets were among the first "bestsellers" the printing press ever produced.*

**历史叙述** — 1517年，维滕贝格的神学教授马丁·路德张贴（一说寄出）《九十五条论纲》，抗议赎罪券之弊。此举本属教会内部的学术争辩，却在数周内被译成德文、印成小册，传遍神圣罗马帝国——印刷术把一场地方争论瞬间放大为全欧事件。路德此后以方言写作大量通俗小册与德文圣经，文字浅白、配木刻插图，销量惊人，他因此成为「第一位畅销书作者」。改革由德意志蔓延：加尔文在日内瓦、慈运理在苏黎世、英王亨利八世为己故另立英国国教。西方基督教就此分裂为新旧诸宗，引发宗教战争、政治重组与教育普及（新教重「人人读经」，推动识字与母语教育），深刻重塑了近代欧洲。
*In 1517 Martin Luther, a theology professor at Wittenberg, posted (or, by one account, sent) the 95 Theses protesting the abuses of indulgences. The act was meant as an in-house scholarly disputation, yet within weeks it was translated into German, printed as pamphlets, and spread across the Holy Roman Empire—printing magnified a local quarrel into a pan-European event overnight. Luther went on to write a flood of popular vernacular pamphlets and a German Bible, plain in language and set with woodcuts, selling in astonishing numbers, and became "the first bestselling author." The reform spread from Germany: Calvin in Geneva, Zwingli in Zurich, and England's Henry VIII founding a national church for reasons of his own. Western Christianity split into Catholic and Protestant confessions, touching off religious wars, political reordering, and the spread of education (Protestantism's stress on everyone reading scripture drove literacy and mother-tongue schooling), deeply reshaping early-modern Europe.*

**史论** — 宗教改革是本图谱「印刷革命」一脉最有力的下游案例，但恰恰在这里，最须守住「使能而非唯一动因」的分寸。把宗教改革说成「印刷术的产物」，是一种诱人的技术决定论：印刷确是关键放大器——没有它，路德或许只是又一个被扑灭的异端（如一个世纪前的胡斯）；但改革的深层动因是宗教的（赎罪券、教会腐败、救赎焦虑）、政治的（诸侯与皇权、教产之争）、社会的（识字市民的兴起）。艾森斯坦「印刷术是变革推手」的论题极具启发，却也因其技术决定论色彩广受商榷——本条因此把 `印刷革命 —enables→ 宗教改革` 一边明确标为 **disputed**，note 直书「使能而非唯一动因」。福克斯那句被选作卷首引文的名言，锋利而带党派色彩（出自新教殉道史观），恰好示范了「印刷—权力」的自觉：新教徒很早就意识到，这台机器是他们对抗罗马的武器。真正的史论分寸，是既承认印刷之力，又不把一场牵动信仰、政治与社会的巨变，简化成一台机器的功劳。（对宗教分歧本身，本条陈述各方、不选边。）
*The Reformation is the most powerful downstream case in this graph's "printing revolution" line—and it is exactly here that the measure of "an enabling condition, not the sole cause" must be held. To call the Reformation "a product of printing" is a tempting technological determinism: print was indeed the crucial amplifier—without it Luther might have been just another heresy stamped out (like Hus a century before); but the deep causes of the reform were religious (indulgences, Church corruption, anxiety over salvation), political (princes against emperor and pope, the struggle over Church property), and social (the rise of a literate burgher class). Eisenstein's thesis of "printing as an agent of change" is highly illuminating, yet widely questioned for its determinist cast—this node therefore marks the edge printing-revolution → reformation explicitly as disputed, its note reading "an enabling condition, not the sole cause." Foxe's line, chosen as the epigraph, is sharp and partisan (from a Protestant martyrology), and neatly illustrates the self-conscious link of "print and power": Protestants saw early that this machine was their weapon against Rome. The true historical measure is to grant the power of print while refusing to reduce an upheaval of faith, politics, and society to the achievement of one machine. (On the religious disputes themselves, this node states the sides without taking one.)*
